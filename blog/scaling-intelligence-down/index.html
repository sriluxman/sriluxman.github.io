Scaling intelligence down

World models could change which machines can afford to be intelligent.

July 10, 2026

Machine intelligence is currently a luxury good.

If a system is big enough — a plant, a fleet, a flagship machine — intelligence can be engineered into it: data pipelines, custom models, integration projects, specialists. The investment pays back only at scale. Every other machine stays dumb. Not because intelligence would not help, but because it does not pay.

That is an economics problem wearing a technology costume.

This is why I watch physical AI and world models, especially the JEPA line of research. Instead of predicting the world pixel by pixel, a JEPA-style model learns to predict what happens next in an abstract representation of the world. The bet underneath is the interesting part: machines that learn how their world behaves from observation, cheaply — instead of being told, expensively, through engineering effort.

A machine with even a modest model of its own process — motion, load, wear, material variance — could adapt and anticipate without a project team standing behind it. Intelligence stops being a bespoke project and becomes a property of the platform.

Commissioning reality will test every word of this. The demos will look ready years before the trust is.

But the direction matters. For the first time, the cost side of machine intelligence is under real attack — and cost is what has kept the machine floor dumb.
