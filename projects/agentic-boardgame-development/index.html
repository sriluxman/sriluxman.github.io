Project / exploring

Boardgame development with agentic AI

Using agents as design partners for rules, balancing, simulation, and the repetitive work around physical game development.

Boardgame design is a useful agentic engineering problem because the output must survive contact with people. A rule set is a system specification that gets executed by humans who misread, improvise, and probe for exploits — the harshest integration test there is.

This project explores agents as design partners across that loop: iterating rule variants, generating scenarios, simulating many plays to find balance problems, and synthesizing play-test feedback into concrete design changes — while keeping human taste, theme, and surprise firmly at the center.

What makes it interesting

The division of labor is the experiment. Agents are good at the parts designers find draining: exhaustive edge-case checking (“what happens if two players trigger this in the same turn?”), tedious balance sweeps, and consistency checks across a growing rulebook. They are unreliable at the part that makes a game worth playing — knowing what is fun — and pretending otherwise produces mechanically sound, lifeless games.

That boundary — automate the exhaustive, protect the judgment — is the same product decision I keep meeting in industrial contexts. A boardgame is just a friendlier place to study it, because a failed experiment costs an evening instead of a production line.

Where it stands

Early explorations: rule iteration and scenario-generation workflows, and first experiments with play simulation. The open question I keep circling is representation — how to describe rules so an agent can reason about them mechanically without flattening the ambiguity that human players actually enjoy.
